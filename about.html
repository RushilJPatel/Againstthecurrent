Protective Factor

Setting Boundaries

Definition: Learning to establish healthy limits and communicate needs effectively protects mental wellbeing.

How It Helps:
  • Prevents toxic relationships from taking hold
  • Builds self-respect and confidence
  • Creates safe emotional spaces
  • Reduces stress and emotional exhaustion
  • Empowers individuals to prioritize their wellbeing

Research shows: People who set healthy boundaries have better mental health outcomes and stronger, more fulfilling relationships.

üõ°Ô∏è

Protective Factor

Positive Coping Skills

Definition: Developing healthy ways to manage stress, process emotions, and navigate challenges.

How It Helps:
  • Reduces reliance on unhealthy behaviors
  • Builds resilience in difficult times
  • Improves emotional regulation
  • Enhances problem-solving abilities
  • Promotes long-term mental wellness

Research shows: Effective coping strategies significantly reduce the risk of mental health issues and improve overall life satisfaction.

Protective Factor

Stable Social Connections

Definition: Maintaining healthy, supportive relationships with peers, family, and community members.

How It Helps:
  • Provides emotional support during difficult times
  • Reduces feelings of isolation and loneliness
  • Offers different perspectives and guidance
  • Creates a sense of belonging and identity
  • Encourages help-seeking when needed

Research shows: Strong, stable social connections are one of the most powerful protective factors for mental health across all age groups.

Risk Factor

Unhealthy Environments

Definition: Growing up in abusive, neglectful, or dysfunctional households where harmful behaviors are normalized.

Why It's Dangerous:
  • Normalizes dysfunctional behaviors like manipulation and control
  • Increases risk of mental health disorders
  • Can lead to repeating toxic patterns in relationships
  • Damages self-worth and emotional development
  • Makes it harder to recognize and seek healthy connections

Research shows: Children raised in abusive households often normalize dysfunctional behaviors and face higher risks for mental health issues throughout life.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Risk Factor

Low Self-Esteem

Definition: Negative self-image and lack of confidence that makes individuals vulnerable to unhealthy relationships.

Why It's Dangerous:
  • Lowers confidence and self-worth
  • Increases vulnerability to toxic relationships
  • Makes it harder to set boundaries
  • Can lead to accepting poor treatment
  • Contributes to depression and anxiety

Research shows: Negative self-image lowers confidence and increases vulnerability in unhealthy relationships, creating cycles of poor mental health.

‚ö†Ô∏è

Risk Factor

Emotional Dependence

Definition: Excessive reliance on others for emotional validation and support, leading to unhealthy attachment patterns.

Why It's Dangerous:
  • Creates dependency on toxic people
  • Makes it difficult to leave unhealthy relationships
  • Weakens personal identity and autonomy
  • Increases risk of manipulation and control
  • Hinders emotional growth and resilience

Research shows: Building emotional independence reduces reliance on toxic people and strengthens mental health, while dependence creates vulnerability.

3 Key Risk Factors That Can Impact Your Mental Health

1

Unhealthy Environments

Children raised in abusive households often normalize dysfunctional behaviors such as manipulation and control.

2

Low Self-Esteem

Negative self image lowers confidence and increases vulnerability in unhealthy relationships.

3

Emotional Dependence

Building emotional independence reduces reliance on toxic people and strengthens mental health.

Why This Matters

We chose to focus on setting boundaries, positive coping skills, and stable social connections as our protective factors, and unhealthy environments, low self-esteem, and emotional dependence as our risk factors because:

1

Peer Influence is Powerful

Teenagers are more likely to listen to and trust their peers than adults. Peer support can be the first line of defense in mental health.

2

Stigma Creates Barriers

Fear of judgment prevents students from seeking help, creating dangerous delays in getting support during mental health crises.

3

Connection Saves Lives

Simple acts of reaching out, listening, and showing care can make the difference between isolation and hope.

4

We Can Make Change

By creating platforms for peer stories, providing resources, and encouraging connection, we're building a movement.
